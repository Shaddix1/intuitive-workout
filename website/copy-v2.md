## PROBLEM SECTION

**Section label:** The pattern

**Heading:**
You've tried. It didn't stick.

**Body:**
There's a recognizable cycle. Someone starts a program with genuine motivation. They show up, they track, they push through the days they don't feel like it. For a while it works.

Then something interrupts it. A trip, a stressful month, a week where sleep went wrong. The sessions stop. And then the worst part happens — not the missed sessions themselves.

The person decides they've failed.

You might have done this two or three times. Each restart costs more than the last, because now there's a prior failure to account for. The obvious conclusion is that you're the kind of person who quits.

But there's another reading: the framework was wrong for you.

**Accent quote:**
The program wasn't designed for *you*. It was designed for an average person who doesn't exist. With your schedule, your history, your body.

**Body continued:**
Only 37% of people who start a structured exercise program maintain it after the first year. The fitness industry's response is to make more programs. Stricter ones. Twelve weeks. Sixteen weeks. "This time it'll stick."

It rarely does. Not because of discipline. Because the question being answered was wrong.

## PHILOSOPHY SECTION

**Section label:** The philosophy

**Heading:**
A different question

**Body:**
I spent years in sport science in Vienna. I studied the methods, learned the progressions, coached people one-on-one through real goals. I was good at it.

Then I left. South America first, then Asia. I watched an arriero on Rainbow Mountain at 5,200 metres move at a pace I couldn't match for the first hour. He was carrying things. He wasn't training. His body had learned what the mountain required and delivered it. For him, this wasn't exercise. It was just Tuesday.

I stood there catching my breath and watched him go.

I'd watched children at a pier in Lima jump into the water without preparation or warm-up sequence, unselfconscious, landing well, doing it again. Movement with no audience. Not performed for anyone, not measured against anything.

I came home with a question I hadn't expected: what if the most effective workout isn't the most optimized one? What if it's the one you actually *want* to do?

Intuitive Workout is built on one observation. The motivation that comes from inside — caring about the movement itself, not a deadline or a number — outlasts every program I've seen. The psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan identified the mechanism decades ago. When the reason you exercise comes from outside you, it lasts as long as those things do. When it comes from inside, it doesn't work that way.

One is borrowed. The other is yours.

**Three pillars:**

**Sustainability over optimization**
The ambition that starts most programs is the same ambition that ends them. The fastest path to results you'll quit is one that ignores how you actually feel. A practice that still exists in five years is more useful than a peak you hit once and couldn't hold.

**Listening over following**
Your body sends signals constantly. Not metaphorically. Literally — the stiffness after sitting, the tiredness that isn't fatigue, the restlessness that wants movement rather than rest. Neuroscience calls this interoception. Research identifies it as a trainable skill. The book teaches it.

**Your practice, not mine**
There is no formula in this book. No program, no phases, no weeks. What's here is enough to find your own approach — whether that looks like a gym routine, a daily walk, or something that doesn't have a name yet.

## WHAT'S INSIDE

**Section label:** What's inside

**Heading:**
What you'll find in this book

**Four parts:**

**Part I — What's Broken**
Starts with Anna. She hit her goals twice. The structure failed to survive her life — not her lack of effort. The adherence research backs her up: most exercise programs fail most people, not because of motivation, but because the framework wasn't designed for actual lives. This part names the problem clearly, without blame.

**Part II — The Journey**
Vienna, the methods, the clients. A pull toward something undefined. Choosing Peru. Joe and Dan in the Andes, moving with purpose rooted in what the day actually needed. An arriero on Rainbow Mountain who didn't train — he just knew what the mountain required. This part is where the question changed.

**Part III — The Philosophy**
What interoception actually is and why it matters. The three types of physical signal that most people confuse — and the different answers they each require. Spine circles. The morning window before the day starts. Listening as a skill that degrades when unused, and rebuilds when practiced. Not mystical. A skill.

**Part IV — Living It**
No program. Actual starting points: pushups, planks, a walk. The two-minute minimum — not a compromise, a genuine floor. The non-routine week where building work is the day's movement. How to begin when you've lost the thread entirely. None of this is a blueprint. It's enough to find your own.

## FAQ

**Section label:** Questions

**Q: Is this an anti-fitness book?**
No. I love training. I still lift weights, I still run, I push myself hard when the moment calls for it. This book is pro-movement and anti-dogma. There's a difference. Chapter Seven is specifically written for people who've been lifting for years and have no interest in stopping.

**Q: Do I need fitness experience to read this?**
None. Whether you've never been to a gym or you've trained for twenty years, the philosophy is relevant. The questions it asks don't require a fitness background to answer.

**Q: Is there a workout plan included?**
Deliberately, no. That would defeat the point. There are examples of how I approach my own training — not as blueprints, but as illustrations. The goal is to help you find your own approach.

**Q: Is this science-based or is it just vibes?**
Both, honestly. The motivation research is real. The adherence data is real. The interoception framework comes from neuroscience. But lived experience matters too, and I don't pretend otherwise. Science confirms what the experience already showed. That's the order in this book.

**Q: Is this a philosophy book or a practical guide?**
It leans toward philosophy, but with practical ground under it. Every idea connects to something you can actually do — or stop doing. Part IV is entirely practical. The rest earns it.
